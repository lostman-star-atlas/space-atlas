1. Introduction & Purpose

DWARF Mini PRO — Space Atlas is not a static cheat sheet. It is a dynamic, predictive planning engine built specifically for DWARF Mini astrophotographers. It replaces guesswork with real-time, location-aware recommendations that adapt to your exact sky, date, and physical constraints.

The Predictive Core: The app doesn't just list parameters. It calculates them. By combining your coordinates, local Bortle class, lunar phase, altitude curves, and atmospheric data, it predicts the exact exposure, gain, filter, and session length needed to maximize your DWARF Mini's output tonight.
🎯 Who is this for?
  • Beginners learning exposure/gain/filter basics
  • Intermediate users optimizing session time
  • Advanced astrophotographers planning HDR splits or challenging targets
  • Anyone using DWARF Mini in Alt-Az or Equatorial mode

Traditional tables ignore reality. Space Atlas embraces it: if a target drops below the 30° imaging threshold, if clouds roll in, or if you only have 25 minutes, the prediction recalculates instantly. You get actionable, physically possible guidance—not idealized theory.

3. Quick Start: Your First Session in 5 Steps

  1. Open the app in any modern browser. Tap GPS to auto-detect location + Bortle estimate, or select your city manually.
  2. Set tonight's date and choose your Bortle scale (1–9). If unsure, start with 5–6 (suburban) and adjust later.
  3. Tap a target (e.g., M31, M42, NGC 7000) or use the search bar.
  4. Review the Data Sheet: Check recommended exposure, gain, filter, and frame count. Note the green altitude window and moon impact.
  5. Open Session Planner → Choose Recommended for auto-calculated values, or ⏱️ I have limited time to input your available minutes. Follow the output to your mount.
💡 Pro Tip: Add the app to your home screen (Share → Add to Home Screen) for instant offline access in the field.

5. Session Planning & Field Workflow

🌞 Pre-Session (Daytime)

  1. Check weather & seeing estimate in-app.
  2. Pick target → run Session Planner.
  3. Read the prediction carefully: Note the exact exposure, gain, filter, and total session length. If the planner shows a truncated window (e.g., "30 min max before target <30°"), respect it.
  4. Pack gear: tripod, balance weights, Bahtinov mask, filters, power bank.

⚙️ Setup (Night)

  1. Level tripod → mount DWARF Mini → balance carefully.
  2. Align polar axis (if using EQ mode) or enable Alt-Az tracking.
  3. Rough focus on bright star → apply Bahtinov mask → fine-tune until spike is centered.
  4. Point to target using app's star map or Stellarium link.

📸 Acquisition

  1. Input recommended settings into DWARFLAB.
  2. Take 3–5 test frames → check live preview:
    • Stars round? → tracking OK
    • Core blown out? → reduce exposure or split HDR
    • Noise high? → verify Bortle/filter choice
  3. Start sequence → monitor first 10 frames.
  4. Log conditions & results immediately after session.

📂 Post-Session

  1. Export CSV log.
  2. Review notes: what worked, what didn’t.
  3. Adjust future parameters based on real results.

6. Advanced Techniques & Post-Processing

🚀 HDR Workflow (Core vs Outer)

Best for galaxies (M31, M51, M101) and bright nebulae with dense cores.

SessionExposureGainFramesGoal
Core10–15 s50–6050–100Preserve central detail, avoid saturation
Outer30–45 s60–70200+Pull out faint arms/nebulosity

Merge: Stack separately → open in Photoshop/Siril → layer core over outer using Screen or Lighten blend mode → mask edges.

📈 Gain & Exposure Science

  • The app suggests starting points, not absolutes.
  • Higher Bortle → higher gain (to swamp read noise), shorter exposure (to avoid sky fog).
  • Always validate with live preview. If stars are round and background is clean, you’re in the sweet spot.

Post-Processing Tips

  • Stack in Siril / PixInsight / DWARFLAB built-in stacker.
  • Separate stars vs nebula/galaxy for independent processing.
  • Apply gentle Clarity / Dehaze in Camera Raw; avoid over-sharpening noise.
  • Use Screen blend mode for star layer over deep-sky base.

7. Data Interpretation & Troubleshooting

📐 Reading the Altitude Curve

  • 🌙 Dark zones = nighttime observation window
  • 🟢 Green zones = best window (night + altitude >30°)
  • Peak dot = maximum altitude + local time
  • ️ 30° rule = imaging below this increases atmospheric distortion & light pollution

️ Common Issues & Fixes

SymptomLikely CauseSolution
Stars trailing / ovalTracking drift, poor polar alignment, windLower exposure, re-check alignment, use lower tripod
Burnt coreExposure too long for object brightnessSwitch to HDR split, reduce exp by 30–50%
High noise / grainGain too high, insufficient frames, high BortleLower gain, add frames, use dual-band if emission target
Poor framing / missed targetPlate solve drift, manual pointing errorUse Stellarium link, enable auto-center in DWARFLAB
App values don't match resultsLocal microclimate, unexpected moonrise, wrong BortleTrust live preview, adjust manually, log actual conditions
🔑 Golden Rule: The app gives you a scientifically grounded baseline. Your live preview is the final authority.
